📖 Literature Survey

Traditional & ML-Based Coastal Erosion Methods

Coastal erosion assessment has historically relied on field surveys, aerial photography, and satellite remote sensing. The Digital Shoreline Analysis System (DSAS), developed by the USGS, is the standard tool for calculating shoreline change statistics including Net Shoreline Movement (NSM), End-Point Rate (EPR), and Linear Regression Rate (LRR). Process-based numerical models such as Delft3D and XBeach simulate nearshore hydrodynamics and sediment transport but require detailed calibration data and extensive computational resources.

Machine learning approaches have gained significant momentum in recent years:

  • Hosan et al. (2024) — Employed gradient boosting and ensemble stacking for coastal erosion susceptibility in Bangladesh, achieving >85% accuracy.
  • Vitousek et al. (2017) — Proposed a hybrid physics-ML framework coupling process-based models with neural networks for long-term shoreline prediction.
  • Vos et al. — Developed CoastSat for satellite-derived shoreline extraction enabling large-scale ML analysis.
  • Beuzen et al. — Applied Bayesian Neural Networks for storm erosion prediction with uncertainty quantification.
  • Mitra et al. (2022) — Used LSTM networks for monthly shoreline position prediction, capturing temporal dependencies.

Explainable AI & Time-Series Forecasting

Explainable AI adoption in coastal science remains limited. Lundberg and Lee introduced SHAP for model interpretation across environmental domains. For time-series forecasting, SARIMA models have been applied to wave height and sea-level prediction. Raj and Gharineiat integrated SARIMA with Random Forest for sea-level prediction, achieving improved accuracy over individual models.

🔍 Research Gap

Despite advances in machine learning for coastal erosion prediction, critical gaps persist:

1

No Interpretable Thresholds

Most ML models lack interpretable erosion thresholds that can trigger management actions. Predictions remain as black-box outputs with no actionable decision criteria.

2

Incomplete Data Integration

Integration of multiple environmental forcing datasets — wave, wind, and current data — into a unified prediction framework remains underexplored.

3

Limited Forecast Horizons

Multi-horizon probabilistic forecasting at operational timescales (6–24 months) is rarely addressed, limiting practical utility for coastal managers.

4

No Spatial Vulnerability

Per-transect spatial vulnerability assessment translating model predictions into spatially explicit management recommendations is largely absent.

⚠️ Research Problem

Coastal erosion threatens approximately 70% of the world’s sandy beaches, causing progressive land loss, infrastructure damage, habitat degradation, and displacement of coastal communities. Current prediction approaches either rely on computationally expensive process-based models or statistical methods that assume stationarity — conditions that rarely hold in complex coastal systems.

There exists no prior framework that simultaneously combines ensemble threshold detection with consensus-based erosion triggers, multi-source wave-wind-current integration, multi-horizon probabilistic forecasting, and per-transect vulnerability mapping in a single interpretable system.

🎯 Research Objectives

This project is structured into four research components, each targeting a specific gap in the current literature. Each component is independently developed and validated, then integrated into the unified CoastalAI framework.

1

Multi-Source Data Fusion Framework

Build a robust data fusion pipeline combining DSAS shoreline transect data with Copernicus Marine Service wave, wind, and current datasets spanning 2009–2024, with monsoon-year temporal standardization and 28-feature engineering.

2

Statistical Feature Selection

Apply rigorous Mann–Whitney U tests with Cliff’s delta effect sizes and 5,000-permutation AUC validation to identify statistically significant environmental drivers from 28 candidate features.

3

Ensemble Threshold Detection

Develop a four-method ensemble threshold detection methodology (ROC/Youden, Bayesian Logistic, Profile Likelihood, Mutual Information) with bootstrap stability validation and Random Forest classification.

4

Multi-Horizon Forecasting & Per-Transect Vulnerability

Generate probabilistic SARIMA-based erosion forecasts at 6-, 12-, 18-, and 24-month horizons with Monte Carlo uncertainty propagation and per-transect vulnerability mapping across 187 coastal transects.

Processing Pipeline

  • Temporal standardization: Monsoon-year convention — Year N = April(N) to March(N+1) — aligning with oceanographic forcing cycles.
  • Multi-scale aggregation: 3-hourly wave data → monthly → annual statistics using Peaks-Over-Threshold logic (maxima, 90th percentiles, storm counts, cumulative energy).
  • Feature engineering: Wave energy proxy (E = VHM0² × VTPK), Stokes drift magnitude, storm wave days (VHM0 > 2.0 m), storm wind days (speed > 10 m/s), current magnitude, cumulative current magnitude.
  • Erosion classification: EPR-based 5-class system using ±0.47 m/yr uncertainty threshold (eroded_high, eroded_low, stable, accreted_low, accreted_high).

📈 Component 2

Statistical Feature Selection

This component applies rigorous non-parametric statistical testing to identify the most significant environmental drivers of coastal erosion from the 28 engineered features.

Methodology

  • Mann–Whitney U test: Non-parametric comparison of feature distributions between erosion (n=9) and non-erosion (n=3) years — appropriate for small sample sizes where normality cannot be assumed.
  • Cliff’s delta (δ) effect size: Quantifies the probability of superiority between groups — Negligible (< 0.147), Small (0.147–0.33), Medium (0.33–0.474), Large (≥ 0.474).
  • Selection criteria: Both p < 0.05 AND at least medium effect size required for feature retention.
  • Permutation validation: 5,000 permutations for AUC significance testing to guard against spurious correlations.

Novel Discovery: Inverse Forcing-Erosion Relationship

All four significant features exhibited lower values during erosion years, not higher. Three features achieved perfect separation (|δ| = 1.000), meaning every erosion year had strictly lower values than every non-erosion year.

This counterintuitive finding reveals that erosion is driven by the absence of protective forcing mechanisms — reduced wave energy prevents constructive onshore sediment transport, and diminished currents reduce sediment supply to vulnerable shoreline segments.

🤖 Component 3

Ensemble Threshold Detection

This component develops a novel four-method ensemble approach to establish consensus-based, interpretable erosion thresholds — no prior work combines these four methods for coastal erosion analysis.

The Four Threshold Detection Methods

① ROC/Youden Optimization

Maximizes the Youden Index (J = Sensitivity + Specificity − 1) on the ROC curve. Bootstrap: 2,000 replicates for 95% CI + 5,000 permutation p-value.

② Bayesian Logistic Inflection

Fits logistic regression with balanced class weights and identifies the P(erosion) = 0.5 crossing point through binary search. Bootstrap: 2,000 replicates.

③ Profile Likelihood Change-Point

Minimizes combined within-group variance across the binary split, analogous to CART optimization. Distribution-free with profile-likelihood interval.

④ Mutual Information Maximization

Maximizes Shannon mutual information I(X; Y) between the binary feature partition and the erosion label. Bootstrap: 2,000 replicates.

🌊 Component 4

Multi-Horizon Forecasting & Per-Transect Vulnerability

This component translates the classification framework into operational forecasts at multiple time horizons, producing actionable vulnerability maps for coastal management.

SARIMA Time-Series Forecasting

  • Stationarity testing: ADF (Augmented Dickey-Fuller) tests to determine differencing order for each variable.
  • Model selection: AIC grid search over 27 candidate models — (p,d,q) ∈ {0,1,2}³ × seasonal orders {(1,1,1,12), (0,1,1,12), (1,1,0,12)}. BIC cross-check.
  • Residual validation: Ljung-Box(12) autocorrelation test ensuring white noise residuals.
  • Seasonal confirmation: STL decomposition confirms annual monsoon seasonality (period=12).

Monte Carlo Uncertainty Propagation

  • 2,000 simulations propagating SARIMA forecast uncertainty through the RF classifier.
  • Produces probability distributions with 95% confidence intervals.
  • Risk categories: LOW (< 30%), MODERATE (30–70%), HIGH (> 70%).

Per-Transect Vulnerability Mapping

Vulnerability scores computed for all 187 transects across 4 horizons using:

Riski = P(erosion) × |EPRi| ⁄ max(|EPR|)
  • 5-tier classification: Very Low / Low / Moderate / High / Very High.
