Project Overview

Research Abstract

An Intelligent System for Coastal Erosion Forecasting and Resilient Infrastructure Planning is an AI-powered coastal monitoring and decision support platform developed with the Coast Conservation and Coastal Resource Management Department of Sri Lanka. Coastal erosion in Sri Lanka is influenced by ocean currents, wind patterns, wave dynamics, sediment transport, and monsoonal cycles. Traditional approaches rely on periodic manual surveys and fragmented analyses, resulting in delayed decision-making. This system transforms reactive approaches into proactive, intelligent monitoring using multi-source satellite data (Copernicus Marine Services, ERA5), shoreline morphology, and environmental variables. The modular four-component architecture covers morphological and meteorological threshold detection, alongside short-term and long-term forecasting with interactive risk zone mapping — reducing analysis time by 85–95% while providing actionable insights for coastal stakeholders.

📖 Research Background

Sri Lanka’s Coastal Erosion Challenge

Sri Lanka has a coastline spanning approximately 1,300 km, encompassing diverse beach types, coral reefs, mangroves, and estuaries. Coastal erosion is driven by a complex interplay of ocean currents, wind patterns, wave dynamics, sediment transport, and monsoonal cycles unique to the region. The Southwest Monsoon (May–September) and Northeast Monsoon (November–February) create distinctly different wave energy and current regimes that traditional monitoring systems fail to capture adequately.

Coastal erosion in Sri Lanka is considered a “silent disaster” — it causes progressive loss of land, damage to infrastructure, degradation of ecosystems, and displacement of coastal communities, yet it proceeds without the immediate visibility of acute disasters. The Coast Conservation and Coastal Resource Management Department (CCCRMD) of Sri Lanka is the primary government body responsible for managing this challenge.

Limitations of Traditional Approaches

Traditional coastal monitoring approaches in Sri Lanka rely on periodic field surveys, manual shoreline mapping, and fragmented data analysis. These methods are:

  • Reactive rather than proactive — damage is assessed after it occurs rather than predicted and prevented.
  • Time-consuming and resource-intensive — manual surveys require significant field teams and cannot provide continuous monitoring.
  • Fragmented and disconnected — wave data, wind data, shoreline change data, and sediment data are rarely analyzed together in an integrated framework.
  • Unable to capture complexity — the complex interactions between environmental drivers under Sri Lanka’s monsoonal patterns cannot be modeled with simple statistical methods.

Machine Learning & AI in Coastal Science

Recent advances in machine learning have opened new possibilities for coastal erosion prediction. Ensemble methods such as Random Forest, XGBoost, and Gradient Boosting have demonstrated strong performance in environmental classification tasks. SARIMA time-series models have been applied to wave height and sea-level prediction. The Digital Shoreline Analysis System (DSAS) from USGS enables satellite-derived shoreline change analysis. Multi-source remote sensing data from Copernicus Marine Services and ERA5 reanalysis provide high-resolution wave, wind, and current variables. The convergence of these technologies creates an opportunity to build an integrated, intelligent coastal monitoring system.

🔍 Research Gap

Despite advances in machine learning for coastal erosion prediction, critical gaps persist in Sri Lanka’s coastal management capability:

1

No Integrated Real-Time System

The Coast Conservation Department lacks a unified, real-time platform that integrates shoreline data, wave data, wind data, and current data into actionable erosion risk assessments.

2

Reactive Rather Than Predictive

Current monitoring relies on periodic field surveys and manual shoreline mapping — a reactive approach that cannot warn stakeholders before erosion damage occurs.

3

Monsoonal Complexity Unaddressed

Sri Lanka’s unique bi-monsoonal patterns create complex, seasonally-varying erosion dynamics that simple statistical models cannot capture effectively.

4

No Multi-Scale Forecasting

There is no existing system that provides both short-term (daily/weekly/monthly) operational alerts AND long-term (multi-year) strategic planning forecasts for coastal managers.

⚠️ Research Problem

Coastal erosion in Sri Lanka is a “silent disaster” that threatens land, infrastructure, ecosystems, and the livelihoods of millions of people along the 1,300 km coastline. The Coast Conservation and Coastal Resource Management Department lacks an integrated, real-time system capable of identifying when, how, and under what conditions erosion occurs.

Current monitoring relies on periodic field surveys and manual shoreline mapping — methods that are reactive, time-consuming, and fundamentally unable to capture the complex interactions between environmental drivers under Sri Lanka’s unique monsoonal patterns. The absence of automated analysis tools means that critical decisions about coastal infrastructure, development, and disaster preparedness are made without timely, data-driven insights.

🌍 Component 1

Shoreline Morphology-Based Threshold Detection

This component identifies the critical conditions under which coastal sediment becomes structurally weak and vulnerable to erosion. By analyzing shoreline geometry and historical change patterns, it detects threshold values that indicate the transition from stable to erosion-prone beach states.

Methodology

  • Satellite-derived shoreline extraction: Multi-decade shoreline position data from satellite imagery, processed using the Digital Shoreline Analysis System (DSAS).
  • Shoreline change metrics: Net Shoreline Movement (NSM), End-Point Rate (EPR), Linear Regression Rate (LRR), and Weighted Linear Regression (WLR) across transects spanning the study area.
  • Shoelace formula: Computes coastal polygon areas to quantify volumetric changes in beach sediment over time.
  • Google Earth Pro & GIS analysis: Spatial visualization and cross-validation of shoreline positions with field observations.
  • Threshold detection: Statistical methods applied to shoreline change rates to identify critical boundary values separating eroding from stable beach segments.

🌊 Component 2

Meteorological-Based Threshold Detection and Prediction

This component examines the oceanographic and atmospheric forces acting on the coastline to determine critical thresholds at which erosion is triggered. A unique dual-threshold approach captures both sides of the erosion equation: the weakening of shoreline resistance and the strengthening of ocean forces.

Dual-Threshold Approach

🌍 Morphological Side

Identifies conditions under which the shoreline structure weakens — reduced sediment supply, lowered beach berm elevation, or narrowed beach width that makes it susceptible to wave attack.

🌊 Meteorological Side

Detects when ocean forces — wave energy, wind stress, and current magnitude — exceed critical thresholds sufficient to mobilize and transport coastal sediments offshore.

Threshold Detection Methods

① ROC / Youden Optimization

Maximizes the Youden Index (J = Sensitivity + Specificity − 1) to find the optimal decision boundary for erosion classification.

② Bayesian Logistic Inflection

Fits logistic regression with balanced class weights and identifies the P(erosion) = 0.5 crossing point as the erosion threshold.

③ Profile Likelihood Change-Point

Minimizes combined within-group variance across the binary split, providing a distribution-free threshold estimate.

④ Mutual Information Maximization

Maximizes Shannon mutual information I(X; Y) between the binary feature partition and the erosion label for information-theoretic thresholds.

ML Classification Models

  • Random Forest: Ensemble of decision trees with bootstrap aggregation for robust erosion classification.
  • XGBoost: Gradient-boosted trees with regularization, optimized for tabular environmental data.
  • Gradient Boosting: Sequential ensemble method for improved prediction of complex non-linear erosion patterns.

📈 Component 3

Short-Term Coastal Erosion Forecasting & Risk Zone Mapping

This component provides near real-time predictions of shoreline changes on daily, weekly, and monthly scales. By integrating real-time environmental forecasts with shoreline data, it identifies high-risk areas before damage occurs — enabling timely intervention and coastal management actions.

Forecasting Approach

  • Environmental forecast integration: Combines real-time wave, wind, and current data with meteorological threshold model outputs to assess imminent erosion risk.
  • Shoreline change prediction: Applies trained ML models (Random Forest, XGBoost, Gradient Boosting) to current environmental conditions to forecast near-term shoreline movement.
  • Daily / weekly / monthly scales: Multi-scale predictions allow both immediate operational decisions and near-term planning over days and weeks.
  • Automated alert generation: When predicted conditions exceed defined thresholds, automated alerts are sent to coastal managers for rapid response.

Risk Zone Mapping

  • Spatial risk maps: Predicted erosion probabilities are mapped onto the coastline, color-coded by risk level (Low, Moderate, High, Critical).
  • GeoJSON / KML outputs: Risk zone maps are exported in standard geospatial formats compatible with GIS tools and Google Earth Pro.
  • Transect-level resolution: Risk assessments are produced at individual transect level for precise location identification.

Stakeholder Value

🏛 Coast Conservation Dept

Operational alerts for field team deployment. Prioritize monitoring resources based on predicted high-risk zones.

🚨 Disaster Management Centre

Early warning for disaster preparedness. Short-term forecasts support evacuation and protective action planning.

🎓 Fisheries Communities

📉 Component 4

Long-Term Coastal Erosion Forecasting & Risk Zone Mapping

This component generates multi-year projections of coastal erosion using ensemble regression and SARIMA time-series models. It supports strategic planning for infrastructure, urban expansion, and coastal protection by predicting future land loss or gain with high accuracy.

Forecasting Methodology

  • SARIMA time-series models: Seasonal Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average models capture annual monsoon seasonality (period=12) in shoreline change data. AIC grid search selects optimal model parameters.
  • Ensemble regression: Multiple regression models are combined to improve long-term prediction accuracy and reduce individual model bias.
  • Stationarity testing: ADF (Augmented Dickey-Fuller) tests ensure appropriate data transformation before modeling.
  • Monte Carlo uncertainty propagation: 2,000 simulations propagate forecast uncertainty to produce probability distributions with 95% confidence intervals.

  • GeoJSON layers: Interoperable format for integration with web GIS platforms and decision dashboards.
  • 5-tier risk classification: Very Low / Low / Moderate / High / Very High, computed for each coastal transect.

Commercialization Potential

The long-term forecasting component forms the core of the SaaS commercialization model: government agencies and urban planners subscribing to annual strategic reports, while infrastructure developers access on-demand multi-year projections for specific coastal segments.

Market opportunity: Sri Lanka’s 1,300 km coastline as the initial market, with scalability to South and Southeast Asian coastal nations sharing similar monsoonal erosion dynamics.
